How I Think

Systems, signals,
and the gaps.

I tend to start with the landscape — not the deliverable. Before I build anything, I want to understand what's actually happening: where the gaps are, what the data is saying, and what it might not be saying yet.

Most marketing problems I've encountered aren't really marketing problems. They're symptoms of a misalignment somewhere upstream — a message that doesn't match the audience, a funnel that moves people through the wrong stages, an automation that technically runs but doesn't actually do anything. I like finding those disconnects.

From there, I use what I know — the metrics, the context, the behavior patterns — to build toward what I don't know yet. I'm comfortable sitting with a hypothesis before it's proven. I'd rather test something and learn than wait for certainty that doesn't come.

The systems I build are meant to compound. A campaign shouldn't just perform — it should teach you something. An automation shouldn't just save time — it should make the next version better. That's the lens I bring to most things.

Start with the landscape

Understand the full system before touching any part of it. Most solutions fail because they optimize a piece without seeing the whole.

Let the knowns point to the unknowns

Metrics tell you what happened. Patterns tell you where to look next. The gap between them is usually where the interesting work is.

Build things that compound

A good system doesn't just solve the immediate problem — it creates more clarity and more capability over time.

Stay curious about the unconventional

The obvious answer is usually already being tried by everyone else. I'd rather follow the thread that hasn't been pulled yet.

Margin

Your thoughts lead. Margin follows.

Not a guided journal. Not a productivity tool. Margin is a place to put down what's on your mind — and gently keep going when you're ready. Always current. Always quietly there.

Margin doesn't just hold your thoughts — it notices what keeps coming up. Open it anytime and you'll find a quiet reflection of what's been on your mind lately. No scores. No analysis. Just a gentle summary, always up to date, always waiting when you're ready.

Visit inthemargin.app ↗

The Story Behind It

The Problem
Most journaling apps push you through prompts, streaks, and structure. But real thinking doesn't work like that. I kept opening apps and closing them — not because I didn't have things to say, but because nothing felt like a natural place to start.
What I Built
I taught myself how to code and used AI to help me bring the concept to life. That process gave me a real working knowledge of how front-end and back-end software functions — and a deep appreciation for what it takes to build something from nothing.
Where It Is Now
Margin is in beta, actively in product and market research mode. Testing with early users, gathering feedback, and refining what makes it different from everything else out there.

What Makes It Different

Your thoughts lead
No rigid structure, no daily prompt you're obligated to answer. You write what's real, and Margin responds to that.
Nothing is forced
Every prompt, every direction, every next step is optional. You can always just keep writing — or stop when you're done.
Built for real life
Two minutes between meetings. A feeling you can't name. A thought that keeps coming back. Margin works with the time you actually have.
Come back anytime
Entries stay with you. Return days later, add more, go further. Nothing expires. Thinking doesn't have a deadline.

I've always been curious about how things work under the hood. Lately that curiosity has pointed squarely at AI — not as a buzzword, but as a genuinely useful set of tools I want to understand and use well.

I'm prompt engineering certified through OpenAI, and have spent meaningful time developing a working understanding of how large language models actually function — the way they process context, generate outputs, and where they're reliable or not.
